一块地，很多种盖法

硕士研究 · 故事版

2020 年，我在伦敦一家住宅开发商实习。团队拿到一块地，要在上面规划一片新社区——楼盖多高、院子多大、能住多少人，都要在方案还很粗糙的时候就定下来。我好奇的是：能不能让电脑先「试」很多种可能，再用数据帮人选，而不是靠手画两三个方案、凭感觉拍板？这篇故事讲的就是我怎么把这个问题拆开、动手验证、以及最后学到了什么。

In 2020, during a placement at a London housing developer, my team had a plot of land and needed early answers — how tall, how open, how many homes — while ideas were still rough. I wondered: could a computer try many possibilities first, and let data guide the choice, instead of sketching two or three options and deciding by gut feel? This is the story of how I broke that question down, tested it, and what I learned.

Why

问题从哪来？

Where did the problem come from?

想象你有一块矩形空地，要在上面盖住宅。各方想要的并不一样：

Imagine a rectangular plot where you need to plan housing. Different people want different things:

  • 开发商希望多盖一些、卖更多套——这直接关系到收益；
  • 未来住户希望房间能晒到太阳、楼与楼之间别太挤；
  • 规划部门要求留出足够的绿地、遵守退线等规则。
  • The developer wants more homes sold — that drives revenue;
  • Future residents want sunlight and enough space between buildings;
  • Planners require green space and setbacks by rule.

这些目标没法同时拉满——楼盖高了，院子可能就小了；院子大了，能卖的面积又少了。常见做法是：建筑师手画两三个方案，团队开会选一个「看起来对的」，等方案做深了之后，再算一算日照、面积够不够用。往往要几周，而且只看了极少数几种可能，缺少「如果当时选另一种会怎样」的对比依据。

You can't maximize everything at once — taller blocks may shrink courtyards; larger courtyards mean less sellable area. The usual path: an architect sketches two or three options, the team picks one that "feels right," and weeks later someone checks daylight and floor area. You only explore a handful of possibilities, with little evidence for what you gave up.

所以真正的问题不是「电脑能不能设计房子」，而是：在还早、还便宜改主意的时候，能不能更系统地看清每种选择的得与失，帮决策的人做更有依据的选择？

So the real question wasn't "Can a computer design buildings?" but: early on, when changes are still cheap, can we explore trade-offs more systematically and give decision-makers better evidence?

What

我做了什么？

What did I do?

我没有试图「一键出完美方案」。我做的是一次有边界的实验：在一块真实项目用地上，搭一套流程，看电脑能不能在几小时内生成大量候选方案，并按我们关心的几个维度打分排序。

I didn't try to "auto-generate the perfect design." I ran a bounded experiment on a real project site: could a workflow produce many candidate layouts in hours and score them on the dimensions we cared about?

  1. 先把问题收窄 不是「设计整片社区」，而是：在这块地的规则内，楼块怎么排、院子多大——哪些数可以变、哪些不能碰。
  2. 把「好不好」翻译成可计算的 checklist 例如：总可用面积够不够、楼间距是否达标、院子日照是否满足最低要求——一共五项，对应客户和规划真正在意的点。
  3. 让电脑批量生成并打分 一次运行能产出上千种排列组合，每种都有分数和 3D 预览；原来手改模型要数天的事，压缩到大约 15 分钟一轮。
  4. 和另外两种常见做法对照 手画方案、以及市面上「填参数就出图」的商用工具——三种路子各适合什么、各卡在哪，一并写进论文。
  1. Narrow the question first Not "design the whole neighbourhood" — but on this site, under these rules: how blocks sit, how big courtyards are — what can move and what can't.
  2. Turn "good or bad" into a checklist Total usable area, spacing between blocks, minimum courtyard sunlight — five metrics tied to what client and planners actually care about.
  3. Generate and score in bulk One run yields 1,000+ layout variants, each scored with a 3D preview; work that took days of manual remodelling shrank to ~15 minutes per batch.
  4. Compare two other common paths Hand-drawn options and off-the-shelf "fill-in-parameters" tools — where each approach shines and where each gets stuck.

实验结论是：技术上走得通——电脑确实能批量产出方案并可视化对比。但我也清楚它的位置：它适合「大方向定了之后，帮你在数字层面比选」；街景好不好看、和周边融不融合，仍然要人来判断。

The experiment showed: it works technically — the machine can batch-produce options and visualize comparisons. But I was clear on its place: best after a rough direction is set, for numeric comparison; whether a street "feels right" or fits its context still needs human judgment.

How

我是怎么想的、怎么做的？

How did I think and work through it?

下面是我实际走通的思路——重点不是用了什么软件，而是遇到模糊需求时，怎么一步步把它变成可验证的事。

This is the path I actually took — less about which software, more about turning a fuzzy brief into something you can test.

  1. 先问对人 跟开发商同事聊：你们在项目早期最纠结什么？答案是「不知道选 A 还是 B 会牺牲什么」——这成了我的研究切口。
  2. 写规则，而不是画终稿 我把「可以怎么变」写成参数（高度、退距、院子大小…），把「不能越线」写成约束。电脑改的是数字，不是我一张张改图。
  3. 做 → 撞墙 → 改 实习期间脚本跑不通、算太慢、结果不合理都发生过。每次卡住，就回到「这项指标到底在回答什么问题」——往往问题定义清楚了，技术解法也跟着清楚。
  4. 不迷信「最优解」 电脑会给出一批「没有明显短板」的候选，但不会告诉你哪条街该长什么样。最后一步永远是人在看：从候选里挑几组，再交给设计团队微调。
  5. 诚实地做对比 手画、自研工具、商用软件——我没有宣称某一种最好，而是列出各自擅长什么、代价是什么，方便后来的人按场景选。
  1. Ask the people who decide I talked to developer colleagues: what's hardest early on? "Not knowing what you give up by choosing A over B" — that became my research angle.
  2. Write rules, not final drawings I encoded what can change (height, setbacks, courtyard size…) and what can't. The machine tweaks numbers, not slide decks.
  3. Build → hit walls → revise Scripts broke, runs were slow, outputs looked wrong. Each time I went back to "what question is this metric answering?" — clearer questions often led to clearer fixes.
  4. Don't worship "the optimum" The computer surfaces candidates with no obvious weak spot — but not what a street should feel like. The last step is always human: pick a few, then let designers refine.
  5. Compare honestly Hand-drawn, custom-built, off-the-shelf — I didn't crown a winner; I listed what each is good at and what each costs, so others can choose by context.

Problem framing Turned a vague "use computers to design" into one site, five metrics, three approaches — something testable.
